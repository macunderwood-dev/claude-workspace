This pattern is not a posture problem. It is a learned response — a habitual way your nervous system has organised itself around ADHD hyperfocus. It can be changed, but not by stretching harder.

What's actually happening

ADHD hyperfocus and the body's bracing response.

During intense focus, attention narrows sharply. The body braces to match — breath shallows, the neck shortens, the shoulders fix, the jaw tightens. This is functional. The problem is that with ADHD, the focus state can last hours without interruption, and the bracing accumulates unnoticed.

01

Tension accumulates below awareness

Hyperfocus narrows internal awareness. You do not notice the bracing building until it becomes pain or fatigue.

02

Pain amplifies ADHD symptoms

Chronic muscular tension increases cognitive load, reduces clarity, worsens brain fog and accelerates emotional depletion.

03

Stretching targets symptoms, not the pattern

Relief from massage or stretching is real but temporary — because the habit of bracing has not changed. The body quickly returns to what it knows.

04

The pattern can be interrupted

With structured training in body-mind awareness, you can learn to notice bracing earlier, release unnecessary muscular effort and coordinate movement with less strain — during focus, not just after it.

The method

Practical self-care skills,
not ongoing treatment.

The work draws on Alexander Technique — an evidence-informed method for reducing habitual muscular tension and improving the coordination of movement — combined with behavioural change coaching and direct experience of ADHD hyperfocus.

The goal is not posture correction. It is developing skills that you use independently: before, during and after focused work.

Body-mind awareness

Learning to notice early signals of bracing before they accumulate into pain.

Head and neck poise

Restoring the natural relationship between head, neck and spine — reducing compression and allowing length.

Habitual pattern change

Identifying and gradually releasing the specific tension habits linked to your focus style.

Push–crash interruption

Practical tools for recognising the build-up before it becomes a flare, and for recovering more efficiently when it does.

Sustainable behaviour change

Building micro-habits that work within the reality of ADHD — not discipline-dependent routines that collapse under pressure.

Lifelong skill

The aim is self-sufficiency — understanding your pattern well enough to manage it without ongoing support.

Common questions

Practical answers.

Do I need a formal ADHD diagnosis?

No. The pattern described — hyperfocus, accumulating tension, push–crash cycles — is meaningful regardless of formal diagnosis. Many people work with me before or without a formal assessment.

How is this different from physio or massage?

Physio and massage address the symptom — the tight muscle. This work addresses the habit that recreates the tightness. Both have value; they work at different levels.

Is this therapy or counselling?

No. The work is movement and awareness-based — closer to skills coaching than therapy. It does not involve psychological treatment or medical advice.

Do sessions require any equipment or space?

All sessions are online. You need a webcam, reasonable internet connection and space to stand and move. No equipment is required.

How quickly do people notice a difference?

Most people notice increased awareness of their tension pattern within the first two or three sessions. Lasting change in the pattern typically takes longer — twelve weeks is a reasonable expectation for meaningful habit change.

What if my pain is severe?

If you are in acute pain or have an underlying medical condition that has not been investigated, please see your GP or a physiotherapist first. This work is appropriate for persistent, chronic tension — not acute injury.
