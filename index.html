Does this sound familiar?

You've already tried the obvious things.
The tension keeps returning.

You work at a screen. You concentrate hard. Hours pass. When you surface, your neck is locked, your jaw is clenched and your shoulders are somewhere near your ears.

You've had massage. You've done the physio stretches. You've adjusted your chair. It helps briefly, then the tension rebuilds, usually faster than before.

  • Neck, shoulder, jaw or eye tension that builds during concentrated work and does not fully clear
  • Tension headaches or eye strain that track with how hard you are working
  • Periods of managing well, followed by a flare that forces you to step back
  • Pain that dulls your thinking and makes it harder to sustain focus
  • A background sense of holding or bracing, as if your body is always slightly prepared for effort
  • Temporary relief from various approaches, but no lasting change in the underlying pattern

The problem is usually not the parts that hurt. It is a wider pattern in the way you concentrate, move, brace and recover. That pattern is not random: it follows recognisable habits. And with the right kind of attention, it can be gently interrupted.

What is actually happening

ADHD hyperfocus and the
body's bracing response.

During intense focus, attention narrows sharply. The body braces to match: breath shallows, the neck shortens, the shoulders fix, the jaw tightens. This is a functional response. The difficulty is that with ADHD, this focus state can last for hours, and the bracing accumulates without any signal that it is happening.

01

Tension accumulates below awareness

Hyperfocus narrows internal awareness. The bracing builds gradually, and you generally do not notice it until it has already become pain or fatigue.

02

Pain compounds existing ADHD difficulties

Sustained muscular tension increases background cognitive load, dulls mental clarity and worsens the kind of brain fog that already comes with ADHD. The two reinforce each other.

03

Stretching addresses the symptom, not the pattern

The relief from massage or stretching is real, but it is temporary. The underlying habit of bracing has not changed, so the body returns to it. This is why the tension keeps rebuilding.

04

The pattern responds to a different kind of attention

This is not about trying harder to relax or correcting your posture. It is about developing the capacity to notice the bracing earlier, and to practise releasing unnecessary muscular effort in the middle of ordinary activity.

The method

Learning to notice and release
habitual tension.

The work is grounded in Alexander Technique, a well-established method for understanding and changing habitual patterns of muscular tension. It is combined with behavioural change coaching and direct experience of ADHD.

This is not about correcting posture or doing exercises. Alexander Technique works at the level of the underlying habit: the largely automatic way you organise your body during effort and concentration. Changing that habit takes time and guided attention, but it is learnable.

Body-mind awareness

Developing the capacity to notice tension as it builds, before it reaches the point of pain.

Head and neck poise

The head weighs around 5kg. When the neck shortens under load, that weight compresses the spine. Restoring the natural relationship between head, neck and spine relieves that compression and allows length to return.

Recognising habitual patterns

Identifying your specific tension habits: when they start, what triggers them, and how they relate to your particular way of concentrating.

Working with the build-up cycle

Understanding the pattern of accumulation before it peaks, and learning to make small adjustments earlier rather than waiting for a flare.

Behaviour change that holds

Building small, specific habits that fit within the actual texture of ADHD life, rather than structured routines that require consistent self-discipline to maintain.

A lasting skill, not a treatment

The aim is for you to understand your own pattern well enough to work with it independently. Not something to return to, but something that stays with you.

Common questions

Practical answers.

Do I need a formal ADHD diagnosis?

No. The pattern described on this page: hyperfocus, accumulating tension, periods of managing followed by flares, is worth taking seriously regardless of formal diagnosis. Many people I work with are awaiting assessment, or have chosen not to seek one.

How is this different from physiotherapy or massage?

Physiotherapy and massage address the symptom: the tight or painful muscle. This work addresses the habit that recreates the tightness. Both are useful, but they work at different levels. If you have tried physio or massage and found the relief temporary, that is precisely the pattern this approach is designed for.

Is this a form of therapy or counselling?

No. The work is movement and awareness-based, closer to skills coaching than therapy. It does not involve psychological treatment or medical advice.

Do sessions require any equipment or space?

All sessions take place online. You need a webcam, a reasonable internet connection and enough space to stand and move. No equipment is required.

How quickly might I notice a change?

Most people notice increased awareness of their tension pattern within the first few sessions. That shift in awareness is itself useful. Lasting change in the underlying habit is a slower process: twelve weeks is a realistic expectation for meaningful progress, not a quick fix.

What if my pain is severe?

If you are in acute pain, or have an underlying condition that has not been investigated, please speak to your GP or a physiotherapist first. This work is well suited to persistent, chronic tension: the kind that has been present for months or years and has not fully resolved with other approaches.
